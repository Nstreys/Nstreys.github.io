History of Gaming Developing

Ralph Baer "Thomas Edison from videogames"

The gaming industry almost simultaneously originated in the United States and Japan. Back in the 1950s, Sega and Nintendo were formed, which then produced only children's toys. But the games themselves appeared in the U.S. as a university experiment. In 1947, two inventors, Thomas Goldsmith Jr. and Astle Ray Mann, filed a patent for the registration of an entertainment device equipped with a CRT tube and buttons that allowed aiming for a point of sight in schematic aircraft figurines. And, until 1962 (when Spacewar came out), on the basis of various universities conducted research in the field, which is very conventionally called gaming.


Ralph Baer, dubbed worked for Sanders Associates in 1966 while working for Sanders Associates and worked with Bill Harrison on the Brown Box, the first home console prototype from which Atari's first home console grew. In 1971, "Brown Box" was modified and renamed Magnavox, and later became Magnavox Odyssey, which was released in 1972. In the same 1972, the newly formed Atari released on arcade machines its famous Pong, designed by Alan Alcorn and Nolan Bushnel, which was the first success of the company and arcade machines in general. Odessey sold 100,000 copies in the first year alone.

Raplh Baer said : "I never knew how to guess the crystal ball, so I had no idea what an incredible pace the chip production would fly forward. But the gaming industry has always been directly dependent on advances in electronics. Moore's law was accurate, but in the sixties no one, myself included, could imagine the consequences of scientific advances. Pure magic!" And he said the main words, words that will put everything in its place : "Were the games better twenty years ago? And the books? Were they better than twenty years ago, too? Video games are an art form, and art is a variety of forms. Is it possible to say that Picasso is better than Rembrandt?" Excellent words!

Having groped a gold mine, Atari in 1974 decided to release a new game every month. Bushnell's strategy involved cloning his own games - Dr. Pong, Pong Doubles, and quadraPong. Other companies also appreciated the approach and began to drive endless clones of sports games such as Handball, Hockey. At the same time, the first text games appeared, such as the adaptation of Dungeons and Dragons - Adventure (1976). This involved a huge number of economic agents - shopping malls organized gaming halls, delivery services, publishers, issued all sorts of waste paper based on motives, etc. only so the wheel of Sansara could continue to rotate, and no one wanted to look for ways to regulate production inside the established system. Especially in 1978, Space Invaders came out, and in 1980 the new console from Atari, sold millions of copies.The day is approaching when the supply will exceed all demand, and this will inevitably be followed by a crisis. And so it happened.

In the United States, from the late 1970s to the early 1980s, it was called the Golden Era of Video Games (that's when a huge number of concepts and ideas were born that propelled the industry for the next decade), the U.S. was preparing for the worst.

"Unfortunately, most distributors expected too high sales, and finished with very high debts."

- Eddie Adlum, Editor of Replay Magazine
